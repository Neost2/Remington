# CarePath Case Study #10

## Marti Cain (Home Health Caregiver — Transportation Cost Burden)

## 1. Participant Profile

- Name: Marti Cain
- Role: Home health caregiver working through an agency
- Setting: Home health agency environment in Central Arkansas; multiple client visits per day across different locations
- Experience: Active home health caregiver navigating multi-client daily routes without mileage reimbursement or overtime compensation
- Segment relevance: High-value signal — frontline caregiver who bears direct transportation cost burden as a systemic consequence of agency compensation structure

## 2. Context of the Conversation

- Method: Public Facebook group response (Caregivers of Central Arkansas)
- Post prompt: "I'm running a small test to better understand the real challenges caregivers face in Central Arkansas. Your insight helps more than you know — even a short comment makes a difference."
- Selection rationale: Direct, specific account of transportation cost burden placed on caregivers by agency compensation structure; operationally relevant to CarePath's coordination and logistics problem space

## 3. Problem Identified (User Voice)

Direct quote:

- "Home Health through agencies don't pay gas mileage, we have to see several clients a day in order to reach 40 hour weeks due to clients only being approved for minimum hours, no overtime pay, and typically unappreciated."

Core issues identified:

- Agencies do not reimburse gas mileage, placing transportation cost burden directly on caregivers
- Caregivers must visit multiple clients per day to reach full-time hours because individual clients are only approved for minimum care hours
- Multi-stop, multi-client daily routes create significant transportation overhead with no compensation
- No overtime pay compounds the financial pressure of high-mileage, multi-client workdays
- Caregivers feel undervalued despite absorbing significant logistical and financial burden

## 4. Impact of the Problem

- Financial impact: Direct out-of-pocket transportation cost for every mile driven between clients; no reimbursement mechanism in place
- Operational impact: Caregivers must optimize multi-client routes independently with no coordination support; inefficient routing wastes time and fuel
- Workforce impact: Retention risk; caregivers absorbing transportation costs while earning minimum-hours-based pay are at high risk of burnout and attrition
- Patient impact: If caregivers cannot afford to travel to clients reliably, patients face missed or inconsistent home health visits — a direct care continuity failure
- Systemic impact: Agency compensation structure creates a transportation coordination problem that cascades into patient care reliability

## 5. Reaction to CarePath Concept

- Not applicable (public comment, not a directed interview)
- Implicit relevance: Route optimization, mileage tracking, and multi-client scheduling coordination are directly aligned with this caregiver's pain points; CarePath's coordination layer could reduce the manual burden of managing multi-stop daily routes

## 6. Existing Tools and Failure Modes

- No specific tools mentioned
- Implicit signal: Caregivers are self-managing complex multi-client routes without agency-provided coordination tooling; no mileage tracking or reimbursement system referenced

## 7. Key Insights Extracted

- The transportation cost burden for home health caregivers is an uncompensated operational responsibility imposed by agency structure
- Multi-client daily routing is a coordination challenge that currently falls entirely on the individual caregiver
- The combination of no mileage reimbursement, minimum approved hours per client, and no overtime creates a compounding financial and logistical burden
- CarePath's coordination capabilities (route visibility, multi-stop scheduling, status tracking) have clear applicability to the home health caregiver segment — not just patients
- Caregiver transportation burden is a leading indicator of patient care reliability; when caregivers can't afford to show up, patients don't receive care

## 8. Validation Signal Strength

- Strong (specific, operational, financially grounded)

Justification:

- Direct, specific account of transportation cost burden with named structural causes (no mileage reimbursement, minimum approved hours, multi-client routing)
- Confirms a previously underexplored dimension: CarePath's coordination problem extends to caregiver routing, not just patient rides
- Adds financial specificity and workforce dimension to the validation corpus
- Consistent with Monica (Case Study #6) and Robin (Case Study #11) on caregiver burden themes
