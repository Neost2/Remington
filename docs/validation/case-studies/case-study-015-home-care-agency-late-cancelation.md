# CarePath Case Study #15

## Home Care Agency Late-Cancelation Dispute (Client + Caregiver Scheduling Impact)

## 1. Participant Profile

- Role: Home care agency operations context (client, caregiver, and scheduler perspectives represented)
- Setting: Agency-based home care with documented cancellation policy and minimum-hour billing model
- Scenario type: Late-cancelation dispute in an active care relationship
- Segment relevance: Strong workforce-and-trust signal showing how policy enforcement can still trigger relationship friction without shared operational visibility

## 2. Context of the Conversation

- Method: Scenario-based validation capture from home care operations workflow
- Trigger event: Client canceled within 24 hours and was charged the agency's 4-hour minimum fee, which had been previously agreed to in writing
- Selection rationale: Captures a common but high-friction edge case where policy, caregiver income protection, and client trust collide

## 3. Problem Identified (User Voice)

Scenario details:

- The client canceled within the 24-hour window
- The agency applied the documented 4-hour minimum charge
- The caregiver lost scheduled working hours from the cancelation
- The client became upset despite prior agreement to the policy

Core issues identified:

- Last-minute cancelations create immediate financial loss risk for caregivers and agencies
- Policy acknowledgment does not guarantee emotional acceptance at the moment of charge
- Clients may perceive fees as punitive when the rationale is not operationally visible
- Agencies are forced into reactive conflict resolution that consumes staff time

## 4. Impact of the Problem

- Caregiver impact: Lost scheduled income and unstable hours when slots cannot be backfilled quickly
- Client impact: Frustration, reduced trust, and possible reluctance to continue services
- Agency impact: Scheduling disruption, administrative overhead, and account-level relationship risk
- System impact: Repeated late-cancelation events can destabilize staffing reliability and care continuity

## 5. Reaction to CarePath Concept

- Strong fit with CarePath's coordination and transparency thesis
- Highest-value opportunity: make late-cancelation patterns visible so agencies can intervene earlier
- Trust opportunity: pair policy enforcement with timeline context and communication trails that explain why minimum-fee protection exists
- Workforce protection opportunity: support caregiver stability by reducing preventable same-day disruptions

## 6. Existing Tools and Failure Modes

- Current approach: signed client agreements and manual policy enforcement by agency staff
- Failure mode #1: documentation exists but is not surfaced contextually at the point of disruption
- Failure mode #2: no shared real-time view of scheduling strain and backfill constraints
- Failure mode #3: agencies lack pattern-level analytics to proactively target recurring late-cancelation behavior
- Failure mode #4: communication is often retrospective and defensive instead of proactive and explanatory

## 7. Key Insights Extracted

- Late-cancelation policy disputes are operational events, not only billing events
- Protecting caregiver income and preserving client trust must be handled together, not as separate workflows
- Transparency around schedule impact and coverage constraints can reduce the perception that fees are arbitrary
- Pattern tracking is essential for prevention: repeat late-cancelation behavior should trigger earlier communication and mitigation workflows
- This case expands CarePath relevance beyond transportation logistics into care-schedule reliability and agency operations health

## 8. Validation Signal Strength

- Strong (high-frequency operational problem with clear workforce, trust, and continuity implications)

Justification:

- Directly maps to recurring care-coordination instability themes in home care settings
- Includes explicit policy-versus-perception tension that documentation alone did not solve
- Shows measurable multi-party impact (caregiver hours, client trust, agency workload)
- Identifies clear product intervention points (tracking, communication, and early-risk visibility)
